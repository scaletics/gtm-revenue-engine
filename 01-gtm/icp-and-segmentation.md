## Purpose

This document defines the ideal customer profiles and segment logic used across sales, marketing, business development, and customer success.

The goal is to focus GTM execution on accounts most likely to buy, retain, expand, and produce strategic value.

## Target Segments

### SMB SaaS

Early-stage or growth-stage software companies with simple buying processes.

Primary GTM motion: founder-led, product-led assisted, or outbound.

### Mid-Market SaaS

B2B SaaS companies with larger buying committees and longer sales cycles.

Primary GTM motion: consultative sales and account-based GTM.

### MSP / Managed Services

Managed service providers selling recurring IT, cloud, cybersecurity, infrastructure, Microsoft, hosting, or compliance services.

Primary GTM motion: owner-led, channel-led, and service-packaging GTM.

## Universal ICP Criteria

A strong-fit account has:

- Clear business pain
- Ability to pay
- Urgent growth, retention, revenue, or operational problem
- Defined buyer or executive sponsor
- Need for repeatable GTM infrastructure
- Potential for expansion or long-term relationship
- Strategic alignment with SaaS, MSP, AI, cloud, cybersecurity, or managed services

## SMB SaaS ICP

### Best-Fit Companies

- ARR: pre-revenue to $2M
- Team size: 2 to 25
- Sales motion: founder-led, self-service, or early sales-assisted
- Buyer: founder, CEO, head of growth, first sales hire
- Pain: inconsistent pipeline, unclear positioning, poor conversion, early churn, weak sales process

### Strong Buying Triggers

- Recent funding
- New product launch
- Founder wants to move beyond referrals
- Low trial-to-paid conversion
- Need to define repeatable sales motion
- Website traffic but weak conversion
- Early sales hire underperforming

### Disqualification Signals

- No urgent revenue problem
- No willingness to pay for GTM execution
- Founder wants only free advice
- No clear target market
- Product is not ready to sell
- No capacity to implement recommendations

## Mid-Market SaaS ICP

### Best-Fit Companies

- ARR: $2M to $50M
- Team size: 25 to 500
- Sales motion: sales-led or hybrid
- Buyer: CRO, VP Sales, VP Marketing, RevOps, CEO, business unit leader
- Pain: stalled pipeline, poor conversion, weak enterprise readiness, inconsistent messaging, long sales cycles

### Strong Buying Triggers

- Pipeline coverage below target
- New market expansion
- New executive hire
- Sales team missing quota
- Product repositioning
- Competitive pressure
- Channel or partner initiative
- Need for vertical-specific GTM

### Disqualification Signals

- No executive sponsorship
- No access to revenue data
- No clear ownership of GTM problem
- Internal politics block execution
- Procurement complexity exceeds deal value
- Initiative is not tied to revenue or retention

## MSP ICP

### Best-Fit Companies

- Revenue: $1M to $50M
- Business model: recurring managed services
- Buyer: owner, CEO, GM, VP Sales, service leader, vendor/channel leader
- Pain: commoditized services, margin compression, weak differentiation, low attach rates, inconsistent sales motion

### Strong Buying Triggers

- Launching AI, cybersecurity, cloud, compliance, Microsoft, or managed infrastructure services
- Need to convert projects into recurring revenue
- Vendor partnership opportunity
- Low service margins
- High ticket volume
- Customer retention pressure
- Desire to move upstream into larger accounts

### Disqualification Signals

- Pure break-fix model with no recurring revenue ambition
- Owner unwilling to standardize offers
- No sales capacity
- No customer base to expand into
- No appetite for packaging or pricing changes

## Segment Prioritization Scorecard

Score each account from 1 to 5.

| Factor | Weight |
|---|---:|
| Pain severity | 20% |
| Ability to pay | 20% |
| Urgency | 15% |
| Strategic fit | 15% |
| Expansion potential | 10% |
| Ease of implementation | 10% |
| Reference value | 10% |

## Scoring Guidance

- 5: Immediate priority
- 4: Strong fit
- 3: Possible fit
- 2: Weak fit
- 1: Do not pursue

## Recommended Prioritization

1. MSP: strongest recurring revenue alignment and clear pain around monetization and differentiation.
2. Mid-Market SaaS: higher contract value and expansion potential, with more complex execution.
3. SMB SaaS: faster sales cycles and useful proof, but lower ACV and higher churn risk.
